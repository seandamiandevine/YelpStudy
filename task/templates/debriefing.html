Thank you for your participation in our study! Your anonymous data makes an important contribution to our understanding of cognitive effort.

The purpose of this study was to invesigate how progress contributes to how people view cognitive effort. Cognitive effort is usually thought of as the amount of mental energy needed to accomplish some task. Generally speaking, effort is usually thought of as aversive -- people tend to avoid spending it in most day-to-day tasks. However, we still use effort all the time because it usually confers some benefit. For example, we may spend a lot of mental effort studying for an exam, because doing so will secure us a good grade.

That being said, there are some situations where people think of effort a bit differently. For instance, when someone plays Sodoku, they do so because effort itselfis rewarding, not necessarily because it gets them any particular reward.

In this study, we hypothesized that one of the reasons for this difference is progress. Depending on the version of the study you are randomly assigned to, you either completed the tasks with a progress bar present or not. When you chose a portal, you were really choosing between two versions of the main task: a hard one and an easy one. We hypothesized that when the progress bar was present, people would choose the harder task more often than when it was not, suggesting that they were more willing to engage in more mental effort when progress was present (like when people play Sodoku).
